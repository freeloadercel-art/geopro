Understanding where and why the famine struck hardest

The Geography of Genocide

The Holodomor (from Ukrainian moryty holodom – "to kill by starvation") was a man-made famine that killed an estimated 3.9 million Ukrainians in 1932–1933. This was not a natural disaster. It was the direct result of Soviet policies under Joseph Stalin: forced collectivization of agriculture, ruthless grain seizures, the closure of Ukraine's borders, and the systematic suppression of Ukrainian identity.

Using Geographic Information Systems (GIS), researchers at the Harvard Ukrainian Research Institute have mapped population losses at the district level, revealing a clear pattern: the deadliest areas were the grain-producing regions of central, eastern, and southern Ukraine—the fertile black soil belt—where Soviet grain quotas were highest and enforcement most brutal. In contrast, northern forested regions (Polissia) experienced lower mortality because they were less integrated into the grain economy.

Why This Topic Matters to Me

The Holodomor is more than a historical event—it is a stark reminder of how ideology can be weaponized to destroy a people. As I learned about the deliberate starvation of millions, I felt a responsibility to amplify their story. This project is my attempt to honor the victims by using spatial analysis to uncover the geographic patterns of this genocide. By mapping where the famine struck hardest, I hope to contribute to a deeper understanding of how political decisions can lead to catastrophe—and why we must never forget.

Testing the Genocide Hypothesis

The Holodomor was not a natural famine. By examining spatial patterns—where the famine was worst and where it was milder—we can test four key hypotheses. Each hypothesis is supported by statistical correlations and historical documentation. Together they provide compelling evidence that the famine was a deliberate, ethnically targeted genocide.

H1: Agricultural Targeting

Mortality rates are highest in the black‑soil belt (Poltava, Cherkasy, Kyiv, Kharkiv) where Soviet grain quotas were extreme. This suggests the famine was engineered to extract grain, not caused by drought.

H2: Ethnic Targeting

Areas with higher percentages of ethnic Ukrainians suffered significantly greater losses, even when controlling for agricultural productivity — a hallmark of genocide.

H3: Industrial Protection

The Donbas industrial region, with its mixed population and vital coal/steel industries, was partially spared due to state rations, showing that starvation was selectively applied.

H4: Blacklist Clustering

Villages placed on Soviet “Black Lists” (cut off from food) cluster in the same agricultural heartland — direct evidence of deliberate starvation.

Legacy of Remembrance

Today, the Holodomor is commemorated through museums, memorials, and educational initiatives across Ukraine and the world. The National Museum of the Holodomor-Genocide in Kyiv stands as the central institution, featuring a symbolic "Candle of Memory" and a vast collection of artifacts, survivor testimonies, and archival documents. Every year on the fourth Saturday of November, Ukrainians light candles in their windows to honor the victims. International efforts, such as the Holodomor Research and Education Consortium and the Harvard MAPA project, continue to document and teach this history, ensuring that future generations understand the dangers of denial and authoritarianism.
